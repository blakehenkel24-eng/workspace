**The SlideTheory Framework**

Every winning proposal follows this structure:

**1. Problem (1-2 slides)**
- Articulate their problem better than they can
- Show you understand the stakes
- Create urgency through inaction costs

**2. Solution Approach (2-3 slides)**
- High-level methodology
- Key phases or workstreams
- Expected timeline

**3. Proof (1-2 slides)**
- Relevant case studies
- Quantified results
- Social proof (logos, testimonials)

**4. Investment (1 slide)**
- Total investment
- Payment terms
- What's included

**5. Next Steps (1 slide)**
- Clear call to action
- Timeline for decision
- Contact information

That's it. 6-10 slides total.

**Why This Works**

Most proposals fail because they prioritize the wrong things:
- They lead with credentials (boring)
- They bury the solution in methodology details (confusing)
- They make the client work to understand value (friction)

The SlideTheory framework puts the client first:
- Their problem
- Your solution
- Proof it works
- Clear next steps

**The 10-Slide Maximum**

If your proposal is longer than 10 slides, you're doing too much.

The detailed methodology? Save it for the meeting.
The company history? They don't care.
The exhaustive capabilities overview? Focus on what they need.

Your proposal's job is to get the meeting. The meeting's job is to win the deal.

## Email 4: Pricing Psychology for Consultants

**Subject:** The pricing mistake that kills 40% of consulting deals

**Preview:** How you present your fee matters more than the number itself

**Body:**

Hi [First Name],

Today we're tackling the most uncomfortable part of consulting proposals:

Pricing.

Specifically, the mistake that costs consultants 40% of their potential deals.

**The Pricing Paradox**

Most consultants present pricing like this:

Phase 1: Research & Analysis - $5,000
Phase 2: Strategy Development - $8,000
Phase 3: Implementation Support - $7,000
Total: $20,000

Seems logical, right? Transparent. Honest.

Also: completely wrong.

**Why It Fails**

When you break down pricing by phase, you invite your prospect to:
- Question every line item
- Negotiate each phase separately
- Compare your hours to their internal costs
- Focus on inputs instead of outcomes

You've shifted the conversation from value to cost.

**The Better Way**

What if instead you presented:

Investment: $20,000
Outcome: A clear roadmap to $200K in annual savings

Same price. Completely different conversation.

**The Psychology**

When you price by outcome:
- The fee becomes an investment, not a cost
- ROI becomes obvious
- Negotiation shifts from "can you do it cheaper" to "what results can we expect"

**Three Pricing Rules**

1. **Single number, not a menu**
Options create decision paralysis. One clear investment for one clear outcome.

2. **Lead with value, not deliverables**
"A comprehensive analysis" vs. "A clear path to $200K savings"

3. **Use investment language**
"Your investment" not "Our fee." "Partnership" not "Contract."

**The Confidence Factor**

Here's the counterintuitive part: Raising your price often increases your close rate.

Why? Because price signals confidence. A consultant who charges $20K confidently signals more expertise than one who charges $15K apologetically.

**The Follow-Up Gap**

Most consultants send a proposal, then... wait.

Maybe a polite "just checking in" email after a week.

Maybe nothing at all.

Meanwhile, 70% of consulting deals are won or lost in follow-up.

**Why Follow-Up Matters**

Your prospect is busy. Distracted. Managing a hundred priorities.

Your proposal — however perfect — is not their top priority.

Your job in follow-up is not to be annoying. It's to be helpful. To make saying "yes" easier than staying indecisive.

**The Follow-Up Sequence**

**Day 1:** Send proposal with clear next steps

**Day 3:** Send a relevant article or case study (adds value, keeps you top of mind)

**Day 7:** Direct ask — "Do you have questions I can answer?"

**Day 14:** Offer a brief call to walk through any concerns

**Day 21:** Final check-in with soft close — "Should I hold space on my calendar for next month?"

**The Golden Rule**

Every follow-up must add value. Not "just checking in." Something useful:
- An insight about their industry
- A case study relevant to their situation
- An answer to a question they haven't asked yet

**When to Walk Away**

Sometimes "no response" means "no." After 3-4 value-adding follow-ups with no engagement, move on.

Your time is better spent on prospects who engage.

**Putting It All Together**

The perfect proposal:
1. Signals competence in 5 seconds
2. Follows the SlideTheory framework
3. Prices by outcome, not inputs
4. Includes clear next steps
5. Is supported by strategic follow-up
